Q: What city has the most skyscrapers in the world?
A: Hong Kong holds the record for the most skyscrapers — buildings over 150 meters tall. It has over 550 skyscrapers, nearly double that of New York City. The density is extraordinary because Hong Kong's mountainous terrain forces construction upward rather than outward.

Q: Which city is the most densely populated on Earth?
A: Manila, Philippines (specifically the district of Mandaluyong within Metro Manila) is often cited as the most densely populated city, with over 110,000 people per square kilometer in its densest districts. The greater Metro Manila area has over 13 million residents.

Q: Why is Venice built on water?
A: Venice was built on 118 small islands in a lagoon starting around the 5th century AD. Early settlers chose the location for defense — the surrounding water protected them from invaders. The city is built on millions of wooden piles driven into the muddy lagoon floor, which have petrified over centuries.

Q: What is the oldest continuously inhabited city in the world?
A: Damascus, Syria, is widely considered the oldest continuously inhabited city, with evidence of settlement dating back at least 11,000 years. Jericho (in the West Bank) and Çatalhöyük (Turkey) are also strong contenders, with evidence of habitation going back 9,000–10,000 years.

Q: Why does Paris smell like bread?
A: Paris has over 1,200 traditional boulangeries (bakeries), which are legally required to bake fresh bread on-site. French law mandates that a "boulangerie" must make its own dough and bake it on premises — so the city genuinely does smell like fresh bread, especially in the morning.

Q: Which city has the longest metro/subway system in the world?
A: Shanghai's metro system is the longest in the world by route length, stretching over 831 kilometers (516 miles) of track across 20 lines. Beijing is a close second. New York City's system has the most stations (472) of any metro in the world.

Q: Why is New York City called "The Big Apple"?
A: The nickname dates to the 1920s when sports journalist John J. Fitz Gerald used it to describe New York's horse racing scene — jockeys called a big, important race "the big apple." Jazz musicians later adopted it to mean a major gig. A 1971 tourism campaign made it the city's official nickname.

Q: What city sits at the highest elevation?
A: La Paz, Bolivia, is the world's highest administrative capital at about 3,650 meters (11,975 feet) above sea level. El Alto, a neighboring city, sits even higher at around 4,150 meters. Lhasa, Tibet, is also famous for its extreme altitude at about 3,650 meters.

Q: How did Istanbul get so many names?
A: The city has been known as Byzantium (founded ~660 BC by Greek colonists), Constantinople (renamed by Emperor Constantine in 330 AD), and Istanbul. "Istanbul" likely derives from the Greek phrase "eis tin polin" meaning "to the city" or "in the city" — used by locals long before it became the official name in 1930.

Q: Which city has the most museums in the world?
A: Mexico City claims the title with over 150 museums — more per capita than any other city. The National Museum of Anthropology there is one of the most visited museums in the world, housing the famous Aztec Sun Stone (often called the Aztec Calendar).

Q: Why is Amsterdam built on canals?
A: Amsterdam's 165 canals (totaling 100 km) were dug in the 17th century as part of a planned urban expansion during the Dutch Golden Age. The canals served as transport arteries for trade goods, and the city's wealth from maritime commerce funded their construction. Today about 2,500 houseboats still float on them.

Q: What is the most visited city in the world?
A: Bangkok, Thailand, typically tops global rankings with 20–25 million international visitors per year. It edges out Paris, London, and Dubai. Bangkok's appeal comes from its mix of ancient temples, vibrant street food culture, and relatively affordable travel costs.

Q: Why is Singapore both a city and a country?
A: Singapore became a fully independent city-state in 1965 after separating from Malaysia. It occupies a single island (plus 63 smaller islets) of about 733 square kilometers. Its strategic position at the tip of the Malay Peninsula made it a critical trading post, and today it's one of the world's wealthiest nations per capita.

Q: What city has the world's oldest functioning street lighting system?
A: Bruges, Belgium, installed gas street lighting in 1848 and later converted to electric. However, the oldest continuously operating street lights in the US are in Boston — some gas lanterns on Beacon Hill have been burning since 1796, making them over 225 years old.

Q: Why is Tokyo so clean despite being the world's largest city?
A: Tokyo's cleanliness comes from cultural practices deeply embedded in Japanese society. There are almost no public trash cans (removed after the 1995 sarin attack), so people carry their trash home. Schools teach children to clean their own classrooms. There are also thousands of volunteer cleaning groups (soji) that maintain public spaces.
