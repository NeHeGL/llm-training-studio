Q: Tell me something interesting about animals.
A: Here's one: octopuses have three hearts! Two pump blood through the gills, and a third pumps it to the rest of the body. Even more remarkable — octopus blood is blue, not red, because it uses copper-based hemocyanin instead of iron-based hemoglobin to carry oxygen.

Q: Tell me something cool about space.
A: Did you know that a day on Venus is longer than a year on Venus? Venus rotates so slowly on its axis that it takes 243 Earth days to complete one rotation, but only 225 Earth days to orbit the Sun. So the Venusian "day" is actually longer than its "year"!

Q: Tell me a fun fact about a city.
A: Tokyo is the world's largest city by population — over 37 million people in the greater metropolitan area — yet it's remarkably clean. Despite having almost no public trash cans, citizens simply carry their trash home. Schools teach kids to clean their own classrooms, and volunteer cleaning groups maintain public spaces. It's a cultural norm, not just a rule.

Q: What's the difference between a meteor, meteorite, and meteoroid?
A: Great question! A meteoroid is a small rocky or metallic object in space. When it enters Earth's atmosphere and burns up, creating a streak of light, it becomes a meteor (commonly called a "shooting star"). If it survives the journey and lands on Earth's surface, it's called a meteorite. So: space rock → meteoroid, in the atmosphere → meteor, on the ground → meteorite.

Q: Why is the sky blue?
A: The sky appears blue because of a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. Sunlight contains all colors of the visible spectrum, but when it enters Earth's atmosphere, gas molecules scatter shorter wavelengths (blue and violet) much more than longer wavelengths (red and orange). Our eyes are more sensitive to blue than violet, and some violet light is absorbed in the upper atmosphere, so we perceive the sky as blue.

Q: How do I remember the order of the planets?
A: A classic mnemonic is "My Very Educated Mother Just Served Us Nachos" — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. Since Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006, the eight-planet version is the standard. Some people still add a "P" at the end for nostalgia!
